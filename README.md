## Abstract

### Background
Polygenic scores (PGS) stratify disease risk but often fail to capture individual variation. "Misaligned" individuals—whose observed phenotypes deviate from their genetically expected values based on PGS—provide a powerful model for identifying factors beyond common-variant effects, including additional genetic factors. 

### Methods
We applied misalignment classification and enrichment testing frameworks to seven continuous traits and three diseases. This allowed us to assess whether misaligned individuals in the UK Biobank are enriched for rare (MAF < 0.1%) damaging genetic variation.

### Key Findings
* **Bone Mineral Density:** We identified significant enrichment of predicted loss-of-function (pLoF) variants in *COPB2* and *GORAB* among individuals with lower-than-expected bone mineral density.
* **Stature:** Shorter-than-expected individuals were enriched for pLoF variants in *ACAN* and *IGF1*, while taller-than-expected individuals showed enrichment for damaging missense variants in *FBN1*.
* **Discovery (Exome-Wide Scan):** We identified 74 significant genes associated with misalignment. Notable findings include *KANK1*, which may have a protective role against primary ovarian insufficiency, and *ACSL6*, a lipid metabolism gene where damaging missense variation was associated with lower-than-expected BMI.
* **Disease Architecture:** Results supported a liability threshold model involving counteracting common and rare variant effects. Diagnosed type 2 diabetes mellitus patients with rare pathogenic variants in *HNF1A* and *HNF4A* possessed significantly lower polygenic risk than those without. Conversely, coronary artery disease controls harboring protective *ANGPTL3* variants had nominally higher polygenic risk.

### Conclusion
This study highlights the power of misalignment-based analyses in complex continuous phenotypes and disease, demonstrating its potential to validate known genetic contributors to traits and identify previously unassociated genes.
